It's Time to Quit the Smartphone

by N. Kostin

Originally written 16 October 2021. Last updated 19 February 2022.

Smartphones are on the next level of frustration. There's a good chance that as you're reading this right now, there's a smartphone in your pocket or at least within arm's reach. Given that smartphones are exceedingly common, it may be strange to profess that they are frustrating. And yet, I'll claim that cell phones are perhaps the most frustrating, if not outright harmful, things to ever be invented.

Broadly, the problems with smartphones (really, with cell phones in general) may fall in one of two categories: (1) technical frustrations and (2) societal and psychological issues that come from using cell phones. The former I'll address first, providing some techical details where necessary, insofar as to not obfuscate the key points. The latter I consider to be a much more potent issue and accordingly will dedicate a longer analysis of.

Technical Frustrations

Let's start by enumerating the things wrong with cell phones from a technical perspective. Feel free to skip this section if you don't think you'll get much out of it, as I consider it the lesser of the two major things wrong with smartphones.

Nobody Really Owns Their Smartphone

For many people, smartphones are, without question, the most important computers they use. People keep everything from their emails, to their conversations with their friends, to photos that capture their favorite moments on their smartphones. Some people go as far as storing passwords and other secrets on these computers they keep in their pockets or purses. So it might sound odd to claim that few people actually own these computers.

Let's answer the important question: what constitutes ownership of a computer? I claim (and I'm right) that unless you have exclusive administrative access to a computer, you do not own that computer. Imagine your employer issued a laptop so that you're able to work remotely. When your company's IT department gave you that laptop, they probably installed all the software you need to work remotely. As such, it is very likely that you don't have administrative access on that laptop. For a company-issued laptop that makes perfect sense: your boss probably doesn't want you playing Minecraft on his dime. By contrast, if a NEET buys the parts for and assembles his own computer, he has the freedom to do whatever he pleases on that machine.

That makes cell phones particularly puzzling 🤔: you literally paid hundreds of dollars for your phone, but unless you've gone through the rigmarole of rooting your phone, you don't own it. Instead, it's really Google's phone (or Apple's phone, if you're a normie or an Amerimutt and have an iPhone). Google has root access to your phone, and they can do whatever they want with your phone, but you can't.

Of course, you can install apps or whatever from the Play Store; this is not the same thing as having root access (or administrative access if you will - I've been using the terms interchangably) to your phone. Have you ever tried to uninstall the camera app from your phone, only to be denied the ability to do so? Well it turns out there's nothing substantive to that: it's not like your Huawei is going to explode if there's no app to take pictures. All that's happening is that your phone is preventing you - a user without root access - from removing that particular app 🤷

At the end of the day, that means that most smartphones are running exclusively proprietary apps on a proprietary operating system. Most people don't know what websites and services their phone is connecting to, let alone what processes are running in the background.

The Bare Minimum

You should have root access to your own phone. You paid for it, after all. Imagine keeping a phone in your pocket all day (where it probably radiates your testicles or something) and not having the basic rights that come with owning any other computer. Specifically, you should be able to

  • Install and uninstall whatever you want. Ideally, this means running entirely free software.
  • Not have anything malicious in your cell phone. No pinging websites in the background without your knowlegdge. No spooky Google services.

(Some may rightly object and say that as long as a cell phone is connected to a cell tower, it is trackable and therefore not private. This is true, but can be mitigated. Use a Faraday cage if needed when roaming.)

All this discussion brings me nicely to my main issue:

It is So Difficult to Root a Phone

I have spent so much time (more than I care to admit) attempting to unlock the bootloader on a number of phones, and I've never succeeded. There's no way I'm that much of a tech-illerate boomer, so that leaves me to conclude that it is just genuinely difficult to acquire root access to a phone.

For starters, when it comes to the choice for an operating system, the options are extremely limited. The nice thing about Android is that the base operating system is open source, and is licensed under the Apache License. That means that, in theory, anyone can make a fork of the operating system without the spookiness that Google includes (not to mention, the layers of spyware spookiness that manufacturers like Samsung and LG add on top of that).

And yet, only a small number of free software, privacy-respecting forks (known as custom ROMs) exist. You're pretty much limited to LineageOS, GrapheneOS, or CalyxOS. And each one of these has a number of problems:

  • LineageOS has great hardware support, but still depends on a number of spooky Google services. For example, LineageOS
    • is configured by default to use 8.8.8.8, Google's DNS server, for name resolution
    • pings connectivitycheck.gstatic.com, a Google's captive portal service, whenever connecting to a new WiFi network
  • GrapheneOS and CalyxOS seem to do a great job of de-Googling, but have extremely limited hardware support. In order to install either GrapheneOS or CalyxOS, your only choice is a Google Pixel. Google's hardware is extremely overpriced for its value: Pixel phones from just a few year ago can easily go for upwards of 500 USD on ebay. To get a cheap Pixel, you pretty much have no choice but to buy a used model from many years ago, and you'll be lucky to get a few months of support on GrapheneOS.

Of course, I'm not making perfect the enemy of good. If you're able to successfully install any one of these three ROMs to your phone, that is immeasurably better than Google's Android that you get on a Pixel. But that's exactly the thing: to successfully install one of these custom ROMs to a phone is such a painful ordeal. It's so easy to accidentally "brick" your phone when trying to flash a custom ROM.

The icing on the cake is that rooting your phone voids your warranty. It's a risk that almost nobody is brave enough to take, and I'm forced to conclude that manufacturers and software developers alike conspire to make it difficult for you to do what you want with your phone.

So what's the soltuion? Honestly, I'm at a loss. The entire process of removing spyware from a smartphone is so daunting, that the best approach is to unironically not own a cell-phone. I fully recognize that this option is not an option for most people (and I'll address this issue later). So that leaves us with the next best thing: minimizing useage of your cell phone.

Smartphones Keep People Tech-Illiterate

There's another subtle issue that's worth addressing briefly: tech literacy. Perhaps this is less of a smartphone problem, and more of a zoomer problem, but I'll take this opportunity to complain about it anyway.

Things have gotten so bad lately that people don't even understand what a file is, let alone basic directory structure. Smartphones have conditioned people to scroll mindlessly through social media

Truth is, when developers automate or hide something in the pursuit of "user-friendliness", they're invariably making something that the end-user simply won't understand. Perhaps the worst offender of this crime is Apple, who touts that their software "just works" 📱

Consequently, you have so many people who

  • Are extemely adept at choosing instragram filters, but can't tell you the first thing about a picture's metadata
  • Keep their grocery or to-do lists in the "Notes" app, but don't know how to edit a text file
  • Only know what a .apk is because of Fortnite.

The list goes on, and only gets more depressing 😔

Social Frustrations

I just spent quite a bit of time enumerating the technological issues with smartphones. These are, of course, important issues that deserve appropriate attention. And yet, all of those problems are completely irrelevant when considering the true problem with smartphones: nearly nobody has sufficient moral discipline.

Smartphones Literally Change the Way You Think

Remember the 90s memes about how watching TV rots your brain? Well it turns out not only were those correct, but it's at least ten times worse for cell phones. Smartphones put your brain in a catatonic state, subsequently destroying your ability to have normal social interactions.

What's the very first thing you did this morning? You woke up, probably unrested from a night of dreamless sleep, to immediately reach for the dopamine hit of your smartphone. Maybe you even slept with your phone right by your ear, allowing its blaring alarm to wake you up.

Throughout the day, you caress your smartphone dozens (if not hundreds) of times in the hopes of achieving even the smallest rush of dopamine. You post a selfie on Instagram and keep refreshing the app to see how many likes it get. You get a sexy picture from your favorite dating app and your mind is immediately shoved into a gutter of lust. Every response on one of your posts, whether positive or negative, makes you feel good.

Normies refuse to part with their phones for any length of time, no matter how short. Deny a normie the ability to check their phone, and they'll quickly grow restless and anxious, not unlike denying a chain-smoker access to cigarettes.

You might object, and say that what I'm describing applies to only the most helpless phone addicts. But the truth is, even light to moderate cell phone usage changes your brain.

Chances are, no matter how much you use your phone, you are dependent on at least some of its functions. Nobody knows how to use a map anymore; Google Maps will get you there quicker. Nobody owns their music anymore; it's much easier to stream it from Spotify. Did something just come up in a conversation that you don't immediately know the answer to? Well you can just ask Siri and she'll know.

All this brings me nicely to my next point:

Why is it literally impossible to find someone nowadays who isn't plugged into their monitoring device 24/7? Normies willingly carry what amounts to an ankle monitoring device at all hours of the day!

Why do people think it's okay to look at their phones it the middle of a conversation? That drives me absolutely nuts (specifically, cashews). Among some friend groups, its not uncommon for nearly everyone to have their phones out on the table while eating. Like here you literally are, with the ability to talk to someone in real life, but insteady you choose to live in the imaginary world of the internet. How does that make any sense?

There are people who go to extreme measures to make their lives more difficult.

Consumerism

Smartphones promote an absurd level of consumerism. How absurd is it that there are people who willingly stand in lines to get the newest model of phones? Smartphone companies have made people way more susceptible to advertising and marketing techniques.

Seriously though, who cares? This is something else that drives me nuts (walnuts, this time). I will concede that having a large battery in a mobile device is a nice thing. But besides that, all the marketing successfully convinces people that they can't live without

  • 90 hertz refresh rates, when 60 is more than enough
  • multiple cameras, when one will suffice
  • glass backs, which have zero advantages over plastic other than a "premium feel"

and a whole bunch of other stuff that nobody needs. All for the modest pricetag of +1000 USD for the newest flagships. The whole thing is just sad 😔

Closing Thoughts

What you're probably thinking:

Did you just shame me for using my smartphone? Umm . . . Wow, just wow. Who hurt you sweetie? You don't like smartphone users? That's problematic. Let's just unpack this - last time I checked, you should just let people enjoy things. And maybe, just maybe, you can realize that there's no ethical consumption under late capitalism. Be better.

Seriously though, I hope I gave a cogent argument as to why I think smartphones are the most frustrating things ever. I doubt I'm persuasive enough to convince anyone to change their cell phone habits. Nevertheless, I had to get these thoughts out there.

Of course, the best thing to do is to ditch your smartphone right now. Life would be so much better if smartphones were never invented. But that reminds me:

You Don't Have a Choice

The worst part about all of this is that society effectively makes it mandatory to own and use a smartphone.

If You Have To Own a Smartphone . . .

As I've already mentioned, the ideal solution would be to not own a cell phone. Unfortunately, we live in a society where that is simply not feasible. So if you have to own a cell phone, I recommend:

  • Using it as little as possible; avoid taking your phone to school or work if you can help it.
  • Use a Faraday cage when roaming.
  • Buying an unlocked phone and checking compatability with LineageOS.
    • If you can't root your phone, the next best thing is using free software wherever possible. You can easily install F-Droid, an app store that promotes free software apps, on any Android phone (rooted or not).
  • Don't buy an iPhone. The claim that Apple doesn't spy on you because iPhones are expensive so they don't need to make money from your data is pure weapons-grade copium. It's the worst of both worlds: proprietary software and overpriced hardware.
